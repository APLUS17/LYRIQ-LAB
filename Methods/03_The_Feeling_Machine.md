# 03 — The Feeling Machine

**Status:** ACTIVE
**Source:** SongwriterCore — *Write Songs That Work* (Part 2) · SongwriterCore 100 Songstarter Prompts
**Purpose:** Five methods for emotional specificity — moving from named emotion to physically grounded, precisely felt experience.

---

## The 5 Methods

### Method 1: The Simile Build
Build comparisons that are earned, specific, and unexpected.

**Structure:** [Emotion] feels like [unexpected but precise physical/sensory thing]

**Process:**
1. Name the emotion's subtype — not "sad" but "the sadness of watching someone try to make you laugh when they know you're not okay"
2. Find a physical sensation that matches it
3. Build the simile from that sensation, not from the emotion label

**Exercise:** Write 5 similes for one emotion. The first two will be clichés. Keep going until one surprises you.

**Resources:** SimileStack (free, similes organized by emotion) · Feelings Wheel

---

### Method 2: The Emotion Mapping Technique
Map an emotion to its full sensory landscape before writing a single lyric. Use the **Mood Compass:**

| Axis | Question |
|---|---|
| Color | What color is this feeling? |
| Texture | Smooth, sharp, heavy, thin? |
| Temperature | Hot, cold, warm, numb? |
| Sound | What does this emotion sound like in a room? |
| Time of day | When does this feeling usually hit? |
| Body location | Where do you feel it physically? |

Write from the map, not from the label. Tools: Feelings Wheel · Emotion Thesaurus (One Stop for Writers) · Feelings & Sensations PDF (Hoffman Institute)

---

### Method 3: The Emotion-Driven Method
Start with the feeling, not the story.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the precise emotional state (use Mood Compass)
2. Ask: what situation would produce *exactly* this feeling?
3. Write the situation as if explaining it to someone who has never felt it
4. That explanation IS the lyric

Key: You don't describe the event — you recreate the feeling the event produced.

---

### Method 4: The 5 Senses Method
Ground every emotional moment in at least three senses. Listeners feel what they can see, hear, smell, touch, or taste — not what they're told to feel.

**5 Senses Scan:** Pick an emotional scene. Write one specific detail per sense:
- See / Hear / Smell / Touch / Taste

Use 3 of the 5 in the lyric. The more unexpected the sense, the stronger the image. *(For grief — most writers go to sight. Go to smell or taste instead.)*

---

### Method 5: The Immersion Frame
Write from *inside* the moment, not looking back at it.

- **Retrospective:** "I remember how it felt when you left"
- **Immersed:** "You're still in the driveway. The engine's running."

**How to shift:**
1. Write the retrospective version first
2. Find the moment of highest emotional charge
3. Rewrite it in present tense, from inside it
4. Don't resolve it in the line — hold the tension

## Emotional Frequency Map (Prompts by territory)

**Nostalgia / unresolved past:**
> "Write about a part of your past that feels unresolved." · "Name something you miss even though it wasn't good for you."

**Anxiety / avoidance:**
> "Write about the area of your life that feels the most fragile right now." · "Write about what scares you about actually getting what you want."

**Love / disconnection:**
> "Write about what missing someone actually feels like to you." · "Write about an ending that never really happened."

**Identity tension:**
> "Write about the part of yourself you edit around others." · "Write about something you pretend does not matter but does."

**In-between / undefined:**
> "What does it feel like when nothing is wrong but nothing is right?" · "Have you ever pretended not to care when you did?"

## Scoring Anchor

Strong Feeling Machine work → **Imagery (30%)** above 0.70.
Critic's test: "Could a reader who has never felt this emotion feel it from the imagery alone?"
Immersion Frame outputs score consistently higher on Hook strength — present-tense urgency transfers.
